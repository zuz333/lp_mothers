Short answer

What is induction of labour?

Induction of labour means starting labour with medical help before it begins on its own. In many cases, the first step is cervical ripening: helping the cervix soften, shorten and open.

This page keeps the definition brief because induction is explained in more detail elsewhere on the website. The focus here is comparison.

Detailed comparison

DILAPAN-S vs balloon catheter

DILAPAN-S and balloon catheters are both used to help prepare the cervix before labour becomes established. They are both mechanical methods, which means they are not based on prostaglandin medication.

The main difference is in how they work. DILAPAN-S consists of small hygroscopic rods that gradually expand after placement. A balloon catheter works by inflating a balloon to apply pressure to the cervix.

In simple terms: both aim to help the cervix open, but they do so in different ways. Your maternity team can explain which options are available where you are giving birth and which may fit your clinical situation.
  • Both are placed by trained healthcare professionals.
  • Both may be associated with discomfort during placement.
  • Both require decisions based on clinical context, not internet comparisons alone.
Detailed comparison

DILAPAN-S vs prostaglandins

DILAPAN-S is a mechanical cervical ripening method. Prostaglandins are medicines used in some induction pathways to help prepare the cervix and may also stimulate the uterus.

From a patient-information perspective, the key difference is that one method acts locally through gradual physical expansion, while the other uses medication. Prostaglandins are sometimes administered in the form of vaginal inserts available under various brand names.

Because prostaglandins may be associated with uterine stimulation, monitoring requirements and the experience of the process can differ depending on the medicine, the dose and the local protocol.

In simple terms: DILAPAN-S is non-pharmacological. Prostaglandins are pharmacological. Both may be part of cervical ripening, depending on the clinical situation.
How decisions are made

How is the method chosen?

The method is chosen after clinical assessment. The aim of this page is to help you understand the options, not to recommend a specific method.

  • Cervical readiness: whether the cervix is closed, firm, short, soft or already opening.
  • Previous birth history: including previous vaginal birth or previous caesarean section.
  • Pregnancy factors: such as gestational age, maternal health, foetal growth and foetal wellbeing.
  • Reason for induction: for example, pregnancy continuing beyond the expected due date or another clinical reason.
  • Hospital policy: not every maternity unit offers every method or outpatient pathway.
  • Your questions and preferences: these matter, but they need to be considered alongside clinical suitability.
Clear, patient-friendly answers

What many mothers want to know

Can the process be paused or changed?

With some mechanical methods, the device can usually be removed by a healthcare professional if there is a clinical reason. With pharmacological methods, what can be changed depends on the medicine, formulation, dose and timing.

Will I need continuous monitoring?

Monitoring varies. It may depend on the induction method, your pregnancy, the reason for induction, foetal wellbeing and local hospital practice.

Will it hurt?

Cervical ripening can involve discomfort, pressure, cramping or tightening. The experience differs between individuals and between methods. Ask your healthcare team what sensations are expected and when you should ask for help.

How long can induction take?

Induction of labour can take several hours or longer than one day. The length of the process depends on cervical readiness, the method used, how the body responds and what happens in the next steps.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between DILAPAN-S and a balloon catheter?

DILAPAN-S is a hygroscopic dilator that gradually expands by absorbing moisture from the cervix. A balloon catheter is placed through the cervix and then filled with fluid to apply pressure. Both are mechanical cervical ripening methods, but they work in different ways.

Is DILAPAN-S a medicine?

No. DILAPAN-S is a mechanical cervical ripening method. It is not a prostaglandin and is not used as a hormone-based medicine.

What is the difference between mechanical and pharmacological induction?

Mechanical methods help prepare the cervix through physical action, such as gradual expansion or pressure. Pharmacological methods use medicines, including prostaglandins, to support cervical ripening and may affect uterine activity.

Can the induction method be stopped once started?

This depends on the method used. Some mechanical methods can usually be removed by a healthcare professional if clinically indicated. With pharmacological methods, what can be changed depends on the medicine, dose, formulation and clinical situation.

Can cervical ripening be done at home?

Some maternity units offer outpatient cervical ripening for carefully selected low-risk patients. This is not available everywhere and is not suitable for everyone. Eligibility depends on local policy and clinical assessment.

What happens after the cervix is ready?

The next step may include breaking the waters, starting or increasing contractions, or continued observation. This depends on your progress and the overall clinical picture.
