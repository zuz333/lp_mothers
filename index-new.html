Induction of Labour: Understanding the Different Methods

Induction of labour is a medical process used to help start labour when there is a clinical reason to do so. This page explains common approaches to cervical ripening, including mechanical methods and pharmacological methods such as prostaglandins.

AEO definition

What is induction of labour?

Induction of labour is the planned medical initiation of labour before it starts spontaneously. It may involve cervical ripening, stimulation of uterine contractions, or both, depending on cervical readiness, pregnancy circumstances and clinical judgement.

  • It may be considered when continuing pregnancy is associated with specific clinical concerns.
  • It can involve preparing the cervix, also called cervical ripening.
  • It may use mechanical methods, pharmacological methods or other clinical procedures.
  • The choice of method depends on individual factors and local clinical protocols.
Main approaches

Main types of induction methods

Induction methods are commonly grouped into mechanical methods and pharmacological methods. Some care pathways may use more than one approach during the induction process.

A

Mechanical methods

Mechanical methods are used to support cervical ripening by applying local physical pressure to the cervix. Examples include hygroscopic dilators, such as DILAPAN-S, and balloon catheters. These methods typically act locally and do not primarily rely on medication to stimulate uterine contractions.

  • Hygroscopic dilators
  • Balloon catheters
  • Other mechanical cervical ripening approaches used according to local practice
B

Pharmacological methods

Pharmacological methods use medication to help ripen the cervix and may also stimulate uterine activity. These can include prostaglandins, sometimes administered in the form of vaginal inserts available under various brand names, tablets, gels or other formulations depending on local protocols.

  • Prostaglandins
  • Medication-based cervical ripening approaches
  • Other medicines used under clinical supervision

Clinical context

Detailed comparison of induction approaches

These sections explain considerations that may be discussed with a doctor or midwife before induction of labour.

Safety considerations

Safety considerations vary according to maternal history, foetal condition, gestational age, cervical readiness and the reason for induction of labour.

According to NICE, pharmacological methods can cause hyperstimulation and require regular monitoring of uterine activity and foetal condition. Mechanical methods also require correct placement and monitoring according to clinical protocols.

Level of control

The degree of clinical control differs between methods. Some mechanical devices can usually be removed when needed, while the effect of medicines depends on formulation, route and dose.

Control should be understood within the full clinical pathway, including assessment before induction, monitoring during the process and decisions about next steps.

Patient experience

Patient experience during induction of labour can vary widely. Some people report discomfort during placement of a device or administration of medication, while others mainly notice cramping, pressure or irregular contractions.

Clinical studies and reviews may evaluate outcomes such as pain, satisfaction, mobility and the need for additional procedures. Individual experience may differ from study findings.

Flexibility of use

Different methods may fit different care settings, hospital protocols and patient risk profiles. Some methods are used only in hospital settings, while selected pathways may include outpatient cervical ripening for carefully assessed low-risk patients.

The suitability of any method depends on clinical assessment, availability of trained staff, local guidance and the ability to return to hospital if needed.

Individualised care

How is the method chosen?

The method of induction of labour is chosen on an individual basis. Healthcare professionals consider clinical factors, patient preferences, available methods and local protocols.

  • Previous Caesarean section: previous uterine surgery may influence which cervical ripening or induction methods are considered appropriate.
  • Risk factors: maternal conditions, foetal wellbeing, pregnancy complications and gestational age can affect the induction plan.
  • Cervical readiness: the cervix may be assessed, often using a Bishop score, to determine whether cervical ripening is needed before contractions are stimulated.
  • Reason for induction: the clinical indication may affect timing, setting and monitoring.
  • Local practice: hospitals may follow different guidelines, formularies, staffing models and care pathways.
Educational product mention

Example of a mechanical method

Hygroscopic dilators

Hygroscopic dilators are mechanical cervical ripening devices. They are placed in the cervix by a trained healthcare professional and gradually absorb moisture from surrounding tissue, which allows them to expand.

DILAPAN-S is one example of a hygroscopic dilator used as a mechanical method for cervical ripening. Its use depends on clinical assessment, local protocols, product instructions and the judgement of the healthcare team.

This mention is provided as an educational example of a mechanical method and is not a recommendation for any individual patient.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions about induction of labour

What is the difference between mechanical and pharmacological induction?

Mechanical methods support cervical ripening through physical action, such as gradual dilation or pressure on the cervix. Pharmacological methods use medication, including prostaglandins, to help ripen the cervix and may stimulate uterine contractions. The choice depends on the clinical situation and local protocols.

Is induction of labour safe?

Induction of labour is a common medical procedure, but all methods have potential benefits, limitations and risks. Safety depends on the reason for induction, maternal and foetal factors, gestational age, method used and monitoring. A healthcare professional can explain the relevant considerations for an individual pregnancy.

Can the method be stopped once started?

This depends on the method. Some mechanical devices can usually be removed if clinically indicated. With pharmacological methods, the ability to stop or reduce the effect depends on the medicine, dose, route and formulation. The healthcare team will monitor the response and decide on appropriate next steps.

Does induction hurt?

Induction can involve discomfort, pressure, cramping or contractions. The level of discomfort varies between individuals and between methods. Placement of a mechanical device may be uncomfortable, while medicines may be associated with cramping or uterine contractions. Pain relief options can be discussed with the healthcare team.

How long does induction take?

The duration of induction of labour varies. It may take several hours or longer than one day, depending on cervical readiness, the method used, maternal and foetal factors, and how the body responds. Some people require more than one step before active labour begins.

What is cervical ripening?

Cervical ripening is the process of softening, shortening and opening the cervix before labour. It may happen naturally before spontaneous labour or may be supported during induction of labour using mechanical methods, pharmacological methods or other clinical procedures.

Are prostaglandins used for induction of labour?

Prostaglandins are one type of pharmacological method used in some induction pathways. They may be administered in different formulations, including vaginal inserts available under various brand names. Their use depends on clinical indication, contraindications, local guidance and monitoring requirements.

Can induction be done as an outpatient procedure?

Some services may offer selected forms of outpatient cervical ripening for carefully assessed low-risk patients. This depends on local hospital policy, the method used, distance from the maternity unit, ability to return if needed and individual clinical circumstances.
